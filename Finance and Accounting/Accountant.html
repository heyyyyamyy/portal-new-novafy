1. What is the primary purpose of accounting in a business?

Answer: The primary purpose of accounting in a business is to record, analyze, and communicate financial information to stakeholders, facilitating decision-making and ensuring financial accountability.

2. Define "accounts" in the context of accounting.

Answer: "Accounts" in accounting refer to the systematic representation of financial transactions related to a specific category, such as assets, liabilities, equity, revenue, and expenses.

3. Explain the difference between financial accounting and management accounting.

Answer: Financial accounting focuses on external reporting for stakeholders, while management accounting is concerned with providing internal information to aid managerial decisions.

4. What is the significance of the "general ledger" in accounting?

Answer: The general ledger is a central accounting record that contains all the financial transactions of a business, organized by accounts.

5. Define the term "double-entry accounting."

Answer: "Double-entry accounting" is a system where each financial transaction affects at least two accounts, ensuring that the accounting equation remains balanced.

6. How does the accounting equation (Assets = Liabilities + Equity) represent the financial position of a business?

Answer: The accounting equation (Assets = Liabilities + Equity) reflects the relationship between a company's assets, liabilities, and owner's equity, providing a snapshot of its financial position.

7. What is the purpose of a trial balance in accounting?

Answer: A trial balance is a list of all accounts and their balances at a specific point in time, used to ensure that debits equal credits in the accounting system.

8. Explain the concept of "cash basis" accounting versus "accrual basis" accounting.

Answer: "Cash basis" accounting recognizes revenue and expenses when actual cash transactions occur, while "accrual basis" accounting recognizes them when they are incurred.

9. Define "reconciliation" in the context of accounting. Why is it important?

Answer: Reconciliation in accounting is the process of comparing and adjusting financial records to ensure accuracy and agreement between different sets of financial data. It is important for financial accuracy.

10. What is the role of an accountant in the reconciliation process?

Answer: The role of an accountant in the reconciliation process is to identify and resolve discrepancies between financial records, such as bank statements and internal ledgers.

11. Differentiate between "Accounts Receivable" and "Accounts Payable."

Answer: "Accounts Receivable" represents money owed to a company by its customers, while "Accounts Payable" represents money the company owes to its suppliers.

12. Why is it essential for a business to manage its accounts receivable effectively?

Answer: Effective management of accounts receivable is crucial for maintaining cash flow and ensuring timely collection of payments from customers.

13. Explain the term "aging of accounts receivable."

Answer: The aging of accounts receivable involves categorizing outstanding receivables based on the length of time they have been outstanding, helping assess the risk of non-payment.

14. How does bad debt impact the financial statements of a company?

Answer: Bad debt impacts financial statements by reducing accounts receivable and potentially affecting the provision for doubtful accounts.

15. Describe the process of recording a journal entry for a sales transaction.

Answer: Recording a journal entry for a sales transaction involves debiting accounts receivable (or cash) and crediting revenue.

16. What is the purpose of the income statement in financial reporting?

Answer: The income statement, also known as the profit and loss statement, shows a company's revenues, expenses, and profits or losses over a specific period.

17. How are expenses classified on the income statement?

Answer: Expenses on the income statement are classified as operating or non-operating, and further categorized as selling, general and administrative expenses, or other expenses.

18. Define the term "depreciation" and explain its importance in accounting.

Answer: "Depreciation" is the systematic allocation of the cost of a tangible asset over its useful life, reflecting its wear and tear.

19. What is the role of a chart of accounts in accounting systems?

Answer: A chart of accounts is a structured list of all accounts used by a business, facilitating the organization and classification of financial transactions.

20. How does the "matching principle" guide the recognition of expenses in accounting?

Answer: The matching principle dictates that expenses should be recognized in the same period as the revenue they help generate.

21. Explain the difference between a debit and a credit in accounting.

Answer: In accounting, a debit increases an asset or expense account, while a credit increases a liability, equity, or revenue account.

22. Define the term "contra account" and provide an example.

Answer: A "contra account" is an account that reduces the value of a related account on the balance sheet; for example, accumulated depreciation is a contra asset account.

23. What is the purpose of the statement of cash flows in financial reporting?

Answer: The statement of cash flows provides information about a company's cash inflows and outflows from operating, investing, and financing activities.

24. How does the concept of "materiality" apply to accounting decisions?

Answer: Materiality in accounting refers to the significance or importance of an item or event in financial statements, impacting the decision-making of financial statement users.

25. Explain the importance of internal controls in accounting and finance.

Answer: Internal controls are processes and procedures implemented by a company to safeguard its assets, ensure accurate financial reporting, and promote operational efficiency.

26. What is the role of the Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB) in standard-setting?

Answer: The Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB) is responsible for establishing and improving accounting standards in the United States.

27. Define the term "working capital" and its significance for a business.

Answer: "Working capital" is the difference between a company's current assets and current liabilities, representing its short-term liquidity.

28. How does the FIFO (First-In-First-Out) method differ from the LIFO (Last-In-First-Out) method in inventory valuation?

Answer: The FIFO method (First-In-First-Out) assumes that the first items purchased are the first ones sold, while the LIFO method (Last-In-First-Out) assumes the opposite.

Answer: A credit memo reduces the amount owed by a customer to a company and decreases accounts receivable.

Answer: Gross profit is the difference between revenue and the cost of goods sold, while net profit is the residual amount after deducting all expenses.
